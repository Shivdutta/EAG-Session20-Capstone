Risk Factors and Uncertainties

Investing in mutual funds involves inherent risks, and it is crucial to understand them in the context of your 10-year, moderate-risk plan. The projected returns are not guaranteed, and the final corpus may vary. The principal risks associated with your recommended 50/50 equity and debt portfolio include:

Market Risk: This is the most significant risk for the 50% equity portion of your portfolio. The value of equity markets can be volatile in the short to medium term due to economic downturns, changes in government policy, geopolitical events, or sector-specific issues. While a 10-year investment horizon provides ample time to recover from market cycles, a severe or prolonged bear market, especially towards the end of your investment period, could result in a final corpus lower than your target. Investor behavior, such as selling in panic during a downturn, can permanently lock in losses.

Inflation Risk: Your investment plan targets an inflation-adjusted goal by assuming an average inflation rate of 5.5%. Inflation risk is the danger that the actual rate of inflation over the next decade will be higher than this projection. If inflation consistently outpaces your portfolio's returns, the real purchasing power of your accumulated ‚Çπ1.71 Crore will be diminished. While the equity component is designed to combat this by aiming for higher returns, the 50% debt component may struggle to deliver significant real returns in a high-inflation environment.

Interest Rate Risk: This risk primarily affects the 50% debt allocation in your portfolio. The value of bonds and the Net Asset Value (NAV) of debt mutual funds have an inverse relationship with interest rates. If interest rates in the economy rise, the value of existing, lower-rate bonds held by the funds will fall, leading to a potential capital loss in your debt holdings. Corporate Bond funds, which may hold longer-term securities, are more sensitive to this risk. The inclusion of Short Duration funds helps to mitigate this, but it does not eliminate it entirely.

Liquidity Risk: This refers to the risk of needing to withdraw your investments prematurely for an unforeseen emergency. While open-ended mutual funds are generally liquid and can be redeemed on any business day, being forced to sell during a market downturn means you would realize notional losses. This could significantly impair your ability to reach your long-term financial goal. Furthermore, some funds may have exit loads if redeemed within a specific period (typically one year), which would reduce your net proceeds.
