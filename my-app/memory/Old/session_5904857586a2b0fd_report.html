7. Risk Analysis

While the revised 30/70 strategy significantly reduces risk compared to the initial plan, several key risks remain that require careful management for a 3-year, goal-oriented investment.

  • Market Risk: Even a 30% equity allocation is exposed to market downturns. A severe, unexpected market crash can still lead to capital loss. While the lower allocation provides a substantial cushion, it does not eliminate the risk entirely. This is the primary reason for the aggressive de-risking glide path outlined later in this report, which aims to systematically reduce this exposure over time.
  • Interest Rate Risk: The 70% debt allocation is sensitive to changes in interest rates. If interest rates rise, the NAV of existing bonds and bond funds can fall. Our focus on Short Duration and Corporate Bond funds helps mitigate this, as shorter-maturity portfolios are less sensitive to rate changes, but the risk is not completely eliminated.
  • Execution & Behavioral Risk: The success of this plan hinges on disciplined execution. This includes consistently making the monthly SIP payments and, critically, adhering to the de-risking strategy. The temptation to stay invested in equities during a bull market or to panic-sell during a downturn can derail the plan. Failure to de-risk on schedule by setting up Systematic Transfer Plans (STPs) reintroduces volatility risk close to the goal date, defeating the purpose of the conservative strategy.
  • Shortfall Risk: The conservative strategy has a lower expected return range (7-8.5%) than the aggressive 11% initially assumed. This creates a risk that the final corpus may fall short of the inflation-adjusted target of ~₹2.35 Crore. This must be addressed by either increasing the monthly SIP amount, scaling back the goal amount, or being prepared for a modest shortfall.

10. Rebalancing & De-Risking Strategy

Portfolio Rebalancing

A disciplined rebalancing strategy is essential to maintain the target 30/70 asset allocation.

  • Frequency: Review the portfolio quarterly. Rebalance annually or whenever an asset class deviates by more than 5% from its target allocation (e.g., if equity grows to 36% of the portfolio).
  • Method: The preferred method is to use new monthly SIP investments to rebalance. For example, if equity is overweight, you can temporarily direct a larger portion of your next SIP into debt funds until the target allocation is restored. This avoids selling units, which can trigger exit loads and tax events.

Mandatory De-Risking Glide Path

For a fixed-term goal, a dynamic de-risking 'glide path' is non-negotiable. It systematically reduces risk as the goal date approaches to protect your capital.

  • Phase 1: Accumulation (Months 1-18): Maintain the 30/70 Equity/Debt allocation to allow for growth.
  • Phase 2: Capital Shielding (Months 19-30): Initiate a monthly Systematic Transfer Plan (STP) to move a fixed portion of the equity corpus into a low-risk liquid or ultra-short-duration debt fund. This gradually locks in gains and reduces equity exposure.
  • Phase 3: Liquidity (Months 31-36): In the final six months, the sole priority is capital preservation and liquidity. Aggressively transfer any remaining equity holdings into liquid or overnight funds, ensuring the entire corpus is ready for withdrawal.

11. Tax Optimization

For a 3-year goal, tax planning is critical as most gains will be short-term.

  • Equity Taxation: Gains from equity funds sold after one year of holding are taxed as Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) at 10% on gains exceeding ₹1 lakh per financial year. The de-risking plan should be mindful of this; ideally, the STP process should first transfer units that have completed their one-year holding period.
  • Debt Taxation: Gains from debt funds sold within three years are taxed as Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) and are added to your income, taxed at your applicable income tax slab rate. Since this goal is exactly 3 years, most, if not all, debt fund gains will fall under STCG. This is an unavoidable consequence of the short horizon and reinforces the need for capital safety over chasing higher, taxable returns.
