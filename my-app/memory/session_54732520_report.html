üîç Definition of Letter of Credit

A letter of credit (LC) is a written undertaking issued by a bank (the issuing bank) on behalf of its customer, the buyer or importer (the applicant), assuring the seller or exporter (the beneficiary) that payment will be made up to a stated amount, within a prescribed time, and against the presentation of stipulated documents that comply strictly with the terms and conditions of the credit.

üéØ Purpose of Letter of Credit

The primary purpose of a letter of credit is to reduce the risk associated with international trade. These risks include:

  • Payment Risk: The seller risks non-payment by the buyer due to insolvency, unwillingness to pay, or other financial difficulties.
  • Delivery Risk: The buyer risks non-delivery of the goods by the seller, or delivery of goods that do not conform to the agreed-upon specifications.
  • Political Risk: Changes in government regulations, trade restrictions, or political instability in either the buyer's or seller's country can disrupt the transaction.

By involving a reputable bank, the letter of credit shifts these risks from the individual parties to the financial institution, providing a level of security and assurance that encourages international trade.

The letter of credit process typically involves the following steps:

  1. Sale Contract: The buyer and seller agree on the terms of the sale, including the goods, price, payment terms, and required documents.
  2. Application: The buyer applies to their bank (the issuing bank) for a letter of credit, specifying the terms and conditions.
  3. Issuance: The issuing bank reviews the application and, if approved, issues the letter of credit.
  4. Advising: The issuing bank sends the letter of credit to a bank in the seller‚Äôs country (the advising bank), which verifies its authenticity and advises the seller.
  5. Shipment: The seller ships the goods and obtains the required documents, such as the bill of lading, commercial invoice, and packing list.
  6. Presentation: The seller presents the documents to the advising bank, which examines them to ensure compliance with the letter of credit terms.
  7. Payment: If the documents are in order, the advising bank pays the seller (or negotiates the credit) and sends the documents to the issuing bank.
  8. Reimbursement: The issuing bank reimburses the advising bank and releases the documents to the buyer, who can then take possession of the goods.

üè¶ Parties Involved in a Letter of Credit

The key parties involved in a letter of credit transaction are:

  • Applicant (Importer/Buyer): The party who applies for the letter of credit and is responsible for paying the issuing bank.
  • Beneficiary (Exporter/Seller): The party who receives the letter of credit and is entitled to payment upon presentation of conforming documents.
  • Issuing Bank: The bank that issues the letter of credit on behalf of the applicant and guarantees payment to the beneficiary.
  • Advising Bank: The bank that advises the beneficiary of the letter of credit's issuance and verifies its authenticity. It may also act as a paying or negotiating bank.
  • Confirming Bank (Optional): A bank that adds its own guarantee to the letter of credit, providing an additional layer of security for the beneficiary, especially if the issuing bank is in a politically unstable or financially risky country.
  • Negotiating Bank (Optional): The bank that is authorized to negotiate the documents and make the payment to the beneficiary. This could be the advising bank or any other bank nominated in the LC.

üìÑ Key Documents Required

The following documents are commonly required in a letter of credit transaction:

  • Commercial Invoice: A document that lists the goods, price, quantity, and other details of the transaction.
  • Bill of Lading: A document issued by the shipping company acknowledging receipt of the goods and serving as a title document.
  • Packing List: A document that details the contents of each package or container.
  • Insurance Certificate: A document that provides evidence of insurance coverage for the goods during transit.
  • Certificate of Origin: A document that certifies the country of origin of the goods.
  • Inspection Certificate: A document certifying that goods meets quality standards.

Types of Letters of Credit

Several types of letters of credit exist to cater to various trade scenarios. Some common types include:

  • Irrevocable LC: An LC that cannot be amended or cancelled without the consent of all parties involved (applicant, issuing bank, and beneficiary). This type offers the highest level of security for the beneficiary.
  • Revocable LC: An LC that can be amended or cancelled by the issuing bank at any time without prior notice to the beneficiary. This type offers little security for the beneficiary and is rarely used.
  • Confirmed LC: An irrevocable LC that is confirmed by another bank (the confirming bank), providing an additional guarantee of payment to the beneficiary.
  • Unconfirmed LC: An irrevocable LC that is not confirmed by another bank. The beneficiary relies solely on the issuing bank's guarantee.
  • Standby LC: An LC that serves as a guarantee of performance or payment. It is used when the applicant fails to fulfill their contractual obligations.
  • Revolving LC: An LC that can be used repeatedly for multiple shipments up to a specified amount and within a specified period.
  • Transferable LC: An LC that allows the original beneficiary to transfer their rights to another party (the second beneficiary). This is useful when the original beneficiary is a middleman or trading company.
  • Back-to-Back LC: A new LC issued based on an existing LC. The original beneficiary uses the original LC as security to obtain a new LC in favor of their supplier.

üí° Recommendations

  • When engaging in international trade, carefully consider the use of a letter of credit to mitigate risks.
  • Work closely with your bank to determine the most appropriate type of letter of credit for your specific transaction.
  • Ensure that all documents presented comply strictly with the terms and conditions of the letter of credit to avoid delays or non-payment.
  • Consider obtaining a confirmed letter of credit if you have concerns about the issuing bank's creditworthiness or the political stability of the buyer's country.
  • Be aware of the fees associated with letters of credit and factor them into your pricing strategy.
  • Maintain clear communication with all parties involved in the letter of credit transaction to ensure a smooth and efficient process.

‚ö†Ô∏è Risks and Warnings

  • Discrepancies in documents can lead to delays or non-payment. Pay close attention to detail and ensure that all documents are accurate and consistent.
  • Letters of credit involve fees, which can be significant. Factor these costs into your pricing and negotiate with your bank to obtain the best possible rates.
  • If the applicant defaults on their payment obligations, the issuing bank may seek recourse from the applicant, potentially leading to legal disputes.
  • Political instability or changes in government regulations can disrupt letter of credit transactions. Stay informed about the political and economic climate in the buyer's country.
  • Fraudulent activities can occur in letter of credit transactions. Exercise caution and verify the authenticity of all documents and parties involved.
