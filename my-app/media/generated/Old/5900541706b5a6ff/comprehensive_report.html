‚ö†Ô∏è 8. Risk Analysis

A comprehensive understanding of potential risks is crucial. While the portfolio is designed for a low-moderate risk profile, it is exposed to several systemic and non-systemic risks. Market Risk is the most prominent, as the 40% equity allocation will fluctuate with broader market movements. A significant market downturn, particularly in the later years of the investment horizon, could adversely impact the final corpus. This is primarily mitigated by the de-risking glide path. Interest Rate Risk affects the debt portion of the portfolio; if interest rates rise, the NAV of existing bonds (especially in the G-Sec fund) can temporarily fall. Conversely, falling rates are beneficial. Sequence-of-Returns Risk is a critical concern for any goal-based plan. This is the danger of experiencing poor returns in the years immediately preceding retirement, which can disproportionately damage the final corpus. The capital preservation phase (Years 8-10) is specifically designed to combat this risk by systematically reducing equity exposure. Furthermore, Inflation Risk remains a constant threat, as higher-than-expected inflation could erode the real value of returns. The inclusion of growth-oriented equity is the primary tool to ensure returns outpace inflation over the long term. Finally, Credit Risk in the corporate bond fund, while moderate due to high-quality holdings, is not zero; a default by a bond issuer could impact the fund's NAV.

üîÑ 10. Rebalancing Strategy

Disciplined rebalancing is key to maintaining the desired risk profile over time.

  • Frequency: The portfolio should be formally reviewed on an annual basis.
  • Trigger: Rebalancing is recommended if an asset class (Equity or Debt) deviates by more than 5% from its target allocation (e.g., if equity grows to 46% of the portfolio).
  • Method: To minimize costs and taxes, rebalancing should first be attempted by adjusting the allocation of new monthly SIP inflows. Redirect more capital to the underperforming asset class until the target weights are restored. Selling units should be the last resort.

This section explores potential adjustments to the strategy based on changes to the retirement timeline.

  • Scenario A: Early Retirement (e.g., at Year 8)
    If you decide to retire two years early, the de-risking process would need to be accelerated. At the start of Year 7, the portfolio allocation would immediately shift to the Year 9 target (20% Equity / 80% Debt). This would mean accepting a potentially smaller final corpus in exchange for locking in gains and ensuring capital safety for an earlier withdrawal date.

    If you choose to work for two additional years, the accumulation phase can be extended. The 40/60 allocation would be maintained through Year 9, and the 3-year de-risking glide path would commence at the start of Year 10. This extension provides more time for the equity portion to compound, potentially leading to a significantly larger final corpus.
